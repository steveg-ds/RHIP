# Community Carcerality and Poverty Governance

This paper investigates rural-urban differences in jail incarceration
rates, drawing from the theoretical framework of poverty governance. We
ask: to what degree do metro and nonmetro counties vary in jail
incarceration and what sets of community characteristics are important
for understanding these differences? The poverty governance literature
centers on how marginalized groups, especially in urban areas are
socially controlled through coercive and paternalistic channels, with
the origins of this treatment linked back to 1970s deindustrialization,
deinstitutionalization, and federal spending cuts. Prisons operated by
state/federal government rather than jails operated by counties are the
focus. We extend the poverty governance framework to rural populations,
county jails and other institutions, providing a comparative analysis
between urban and rural communities. Our analysis is based on primary
and secondary data and employs state fixed-effects linear regression for
approximately 1,400 counties. The outcome variable is county-level jail
incarceration. We identify independent variables that capture factors
denoted in the poverty governance framework: attributes of marginalized
populations (poverty, unemployment, the share of Black and Hispanic);
governmental institutions (e.g. county government administrative and
resource capacity measures, policy priorities and programming); and
local political orientations. We also include control variables
including local crime rates.

Preliminary findings indicate that different sets of factors are
associated with jail incarceration in metro, nonmetro adjacent, and
nonmetro nonadjacent (remote rural) counties. In metro counties,
property crime alone is more closely connected to jail incarceration
rather than to marginalized populations, local government and
politics. By contrast, key factors denoted in the poverty governance
framework appear more important in rural areas: here marginalized
populations, government, politics, and religion contribute to
incarceration over and above crime rates. For example, in nonmetro
adjacent counties, the share of Hispanics, political and religious
affiliation, and county government capacity are more closely linked to
jail incarceration. For remote rural counties, a higher poverty
population and fiscal capacity are more closely linked to incarceration.
These findings suggest different mechanisms at play in each spatial
context that contribute to incarceration, be it discriminatory policies
or the capacity of local governments to offer diversionary
programming---important findings for both scholarly and practitioner
communities.
